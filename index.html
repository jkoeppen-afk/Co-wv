5 Reasons West Virginia Was Left Behind

1.  No Renewable Buffer Against Price Swings

Colorado built enough wind and solar that when fossil fuel prices spiked nationally, renewables absorbed demand and softened the blow. With only 4% renewables, WV had no cushion — every gas and coal price shock hit ratepayers directly and in full.

2.  An Ancient Coal Fleet That Customers Are Paying to Keep Alive

West Virginia's coal fleet is mostly 50+ years old. Rather than retiring these plants, the Public Service Commission allowed utilities to charge customers hundreds of millions in compliance upgrades to keep them running potentially until 2040. New wind, solar, and battery storage would be significantly cheaper.

3.  Trapped on the PJM Grid With No Leverage

Colorado is on the Western grid (WECC) and avoided PJM's catastrophic capacity auction spikes entirely. West Virginia is fully embedded in PJM — so when PJM's wholesale prices jumped 833% in a single year, WV utilities had to pass every dollar of that cost directly to customers who had no alternatives.

4.  Shrinking Population — the Hidden Cost Multiplier

When people leave a state, the same fixed infrastructure costs — power lines, substations, aging plants — get divided among fewer ratepayers. Each remaining customer absorbs a larger share of a system built for a much bigger population. WV is the only state where median inflation-adjusted household income was lower in 2023 than in 1970.

5.  Regulatory Capture

West Virginia's rate-approval commission — the body that approves all utility rate hikes — includes a former power company lobbyist and the former head of the state coal association, both appointed by the governor. Critics argue this makes meaningful cost oversight nearly impossible, and utilities routinely receive the rate increases they request.

The Bottom Line

West Virginia sits on top of enormous natural gas reserves but uses gas for only 6% of its electricity. It imports expensive power through PJM, pays to keep 50-year-old coal plants alive, and has a regulatory board stacked with former industry insiders — while states that moved to cheaper generation years ago pay significantly less.

Colorado's renewable investment shows the better path: states that replaced aging coal with cheaper modern generation are now paying less — not more. The market has delivered its verdict. The question for West Virginia is how long the political will to ignore it can last.
